Bone Density Scan Overview

A bone density scan, also known as dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA or DXA), is a highly accurate, non-invasive imaging test designed to measure bone mineral density (BMD). By scanning key areas of the skeletal structure—typically the lower spine (lumbar spine) and the hips—this scan provides vital insights into your overall bone strength and health.

DEXA scans are the gold standard for diagnosing osteoporosis and assessing a patient's risk of future fractures. The test works by sending two low-energy X-ray beams through the bones. The amount of radiation used is extremely small—less than one-tenth of the dose of a standard chest X-ray, making it a safe, simple, and painless preventative health screen.

Why Choose a DEXA Scan?

Proactive screening plays a critical role in preserving skeletal health. As we age, bones can naturally lose density and become brittle, often without any outward warning signs. A Bone Density Scan enables early intervention and customized preventative treatment plans.

  • Early Detection of Osteopenia (early stage bone loss) and Osteoporosis
  • Accurate Assessment of Bone Fracture Risks
  • Monitors the Effectiveness of Ongoing Osteoporosis Treatments
  • Low-Dose Radiation—safer than conventional X-ray methods
  • Quick and Painless—requires no anesthesia, incisions, or downtime

The Procedure & Preparation

Before the Scan (Preparation):

DEXA scans require very little preparation. However, to ensure accurate readings, please follow these guidelines:

  • Do not take calcium supplements for at least 24 hours prior to your scan.
  • Wear loose, comfortable clothing. Avoid wearing garments with metal buttons, snaps, zippers, or buckles.
  • Inform your technologist if you have recently had a barium exam, CT scan with contrast, or a nuclear medicine scan.
  • Let us know if there is any possibility that you might be pregnant.

During the Scan:

You will lie comfortably on a padded examination table. A scanning arm will slowly pass overhead, directing low-dose energy beams through your body. The technologist will position your legs or spine to capture the correct diagnostic angles. The entire process takes only 10 to 20 minutes, during which you will need to lie still for short periods.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How often should I get a Bone Density Scan?

A: For individuals with high risk factors or those monitoring osteoporosis treatment, doctors typically recommend a scan every 1 to 2 years. Women over 65 and men over 70 should discuss screening schedules with their physicians.

Q: Is the scan painful?

A: No, a DEXA scan is entirely painless. It is a non-invasive scan, meaning no needles, injections, or discomfort are involved.

Q: What do my scores mean?

A: Your results are reported as a T-score, which compares your bone density to a healthy young adult. A T-score of -1.0 or higher is normal. A T-score between -1.0 and -2.5 indicates osteopenia (low bone density). A score of -2.5 or lower indicates osteoporosis.
